Predicting Employee Turnover: A Data Analysis of HR Employee Churn

Brenda Murillo
Department of Computer Information Systems & Data Analytics  |  California State University, Bakersfield

Introduction

Employee turnover is a costly challenge for organizations — replacing a single employee can cost 50–200% of their annual salary in recruiting, onboarding, and lost productivity.

With advances in data analytics, HR departments can now identify at-risk employees before they leave and implement targeted retention interventions.

This study analyzes the HR Employee Churn dataset to uncover the primary drivers of voluntary departure using descriptive statistics, correlation analysis, and visualization.

Research Question & Hypothesis

Question: What are the primary factors associated with voluntary employee turnover, and how can descriptive analytics reveal actionable retention strategies?


Hypothesis: Employee satisfaction level, salary category, and tenure are the primary predictors of turnover, with low-satisfaction and low-salary employees exhibiting the highest attrition rates.

Key Findings

Finding 1 — Turnover Rate: 23.81% of employees left — approximately 1 in 4 — representing a significant and recurring organizational cost.

Finding 2 — Satisfaction: Employees who left averaged a satisfaction score of 0.440 vs. 0.667 for those who stayed (r = −0.388). The strongest single predictor in the dataset.

Finding 3 — Salary: Low-salary employees leave at 29.7% — more than 4× the rate of high-salary employees (6.6%). Compensation is a critical retention lever.

Finding 4 — Tenure: The 3–5 year window is the highest-risk attrition period (r = 0.145), consistent with the "mid-career itch" pattern in organizational behavior research.

Conclusion

Three primary drivers of turnover were identified:

  • Low satisfaction — dominant predictor (r = −0.388); invest in engagement programs and regular pulse surveys
  • Low compensation — 4.5× higher attrition vs. high salary; competitive pay structures are essential
  • Mid-career tenure (3–5 yrs) — highest-risk window; targeted career development and mentorship recommended

Combining satisfaction monitoring with compensation benchmarking and structured advancement paths offers the strongest path to reducing attrition.

Future Research

  • Apply ML models (logistic regression, random forest, XGBoost) for predictive attrition classification
  • Incorporate department & job role variables for sector-level analysis
  • Test on larger, real-world datasets to validate generalizability
  • Add engagement survey and manager effectiveness scores as predictors
