Predicting Employee Turnover: A Data Analysis of HR Employee Churn

Brenda Murillo

Department of Computer Information Systems & Data Analytics
California State University, Bakersfield

Abstract

Employee turnover is one of the most costly challenges facing modern organizations. This study presents a comprehensive data analysis of an HR employee churn dataset containing 14,999 records across 10 variables to identify the key drivers behind voluntary employee departure. Through descriptive statistics, data cleaning, correlation analysis, and visualization, this paper reveals that employee satisfaction level is the strongest predictor of turnover (r = −0.388), followed by tenure and salary category. Employees who left reported a mean satisfaction level of 0.44 compared to 0.667 for those who stayed. Additionally, low-salary employees experienced a 29.7% turnover rate—more than four times that of high-salary employees (6.6%). These findings provide actionable insights for HR professionals seeking to develop data-driven retention strategies.

1. Introduction

Employee turnover represents a significant financial and operational burden for organizations across all industries. The costs associated with recruiting, onboarding, and training replacement employees can range from 50% to 200% of an employee's annual salary (Boushey & Glynn, 2012). Beyond direct financial costs, high turnover disrupts team dynamics, reduces organizational knowledge retention, and negatively impacts customer service quality.

With the rise of big data and advanced analytics tools, human resources departments now have the ability to leverage workforce data to predict attrition before it occurs. Predictive HR analytics can help organizations identify at-risk employees and implement targeted interventions to improve retention rates.

This study analyzes an HR employee churn dataset to answer the following research question: What are the primary factors associated with employee voluntary turnover, and how can descriptive analytics reveal patterns that inform retention strategies?

The hypothesis guiding this investigation is that employee satisfaction level, salary category, and tenure are the primary predictors of employee turnover, with low-satisfaction and low-salary employees exhibiting the highest attrition rates.

2. Dataset Description

2.1 Data Source

The dataset used in this analysis is the HR Employee Churn dataset, a widely used benchmark dataset in human resources analytics. It contains simulated HR records designed to represent realistic workforce data patterns. The dataset was accessed via a zipped CSV archive (hr_employee_churn_data.csv) containing 14,999 employee records.

3. Methodology

3.1 Data Preparation

The dataset was loaded using the Pandas library in Python. Initial exploration confirmed 14,999 records across 10 columns. Data types were validated: seven integer columns, two float columns, and one categorical (object) column for salary.

3.2 Data Cleaning

A missing value analysis was conducted using df.isnull().sum(). Only two missing values were identified, both in the satisfaction_level column. These were imputed using the column mean (0.613) to preserve dataset integrity while minimizing bias. No duplicate records were detected. Data type validation confirmed all columns matched their expected types.

Outlier detection was performed using descriptive statistics. The average_montly_hours variable showed a range of 96–310 hours, with employees in the top decile averaging over 267 hours per month — approximately 67 hours per week — suggesting a subset of highly overworked employees. Similarly, time_spend_company had a maximum of 10 years, with the 90th percentile at 5 years, indicating right-skew in tenure distribution.

3.3 Descriptive Statistical Analysis

Comprehensive descriptive statistics were computed for all numerical variables, including measures of central tendency (mean, median, mode), dispersion (variance, standard deviation, range), and distribution shape (quartiles and percentiles at the 10th through 90th percentile levels).

3.4 Comparative and Correlation Analysis

To identify factors associated with employee turnover, the dataset was segmented by the target variable (left). Mean values were compared between employees who left and those who stayed across all numerical features. Pearson correlation coefficients were computed between each feature and the binary turnover indicator. Turnover rates were also computed by salary category and by years of tenure.

4.2 Employee Turnover Overview

Of the 14,999 employees in the dataset, 3,571 (23.81%) left the company. This turnover rate is notably higher than the U.S. average voluntary separation rate, which typically ranges between 15–20%, suggesting elevated attrition in the simulated organization.

Key Finding: 23.81% of employees in this dataset voluntarily left — approximately 1 in 4 employees. This level of turnover represents a significant organizational risk and financial burden.

4.3 Satisfaction Level as the Primary Driver

The most striking disparity between retained and departed employees was observed in satisfaction level. Employees who left reported an average satisfaction score of 0.440, compared to 0.667 for employees who stayed — a difference of 0.227 points (34% lower). The Pearson correlation between satisfaction level and turnover was r = −0.388, the strongest predictor in the dataset.

Key Finding: Employees who left had an average satisfaction level of 0.44 vs. 0.667 for those who stayed (r = −0.388). Satisfaction level is the single strongest predictor of voluntary turnover in this dataset.

4.5 Work Hours, Tenure, and Other Factors

Employees who left worked an average of 207.4 monthly hours compared to 199.1 for those who stayed, suggesting moderate overwork as a contributing factor. Tenure showed a moderate positive correlation with turnover (r = 0.145), with employees in their 3rd–5th year at the company showing the highest departure rates — a pattern consistent with the "3-year itch" phenomenon observed in organizational behavior research.

Counterintuitively, employees who experienced a workplace accident had a significantly lower turnover rate (4.73%) compared to those who did not (17.5%), producing a negative correlation of r = −0.155. This may reflect that employees who experienced a workplace incident remained due to ongoing medical support, workers' compensation, or increased loyalty to the organization. Promotion within the last 5 years also showed a protective effect against turnover, with only 0.53% of leavers having received a recent promotion versus 2.63% of those who stayed.

5. Conclusion

This analysis of the HR Employee Churn dataset provides clear evidence that employee satisfaction, compensation level, and tenure are the primary factors associated with voluntary turnover. The 23.81% overall attrition rate, driven largely by dissatisfied and underpaid employees, represents a significant organizational challenge that data analytics can help address.

The key findings are threefold. First, employee satisfaction is the dominant predictor of turnover, with departing employees reporting satisfaction levels 34% lower than retained employees (r = −0.388). Organizations should invest in regular satisfaction surveys and proactive engagement programs. Second, compensation is a powerful retention lever: low-salary employees leave at a rate 4.5 times higher than high-salary employees. Competitive and equitable pay structures are essential for retention. Third, mid-career employees (3–5 years) are the most at-risk group, suggesting the need for targeted career development, mentorship, and advancement opportunities at this critical tenure juncture.

For future research, it is recommended to incorporate additional variables such as department, job role, manager quality, and remote work status. Machine learning models — including logistic regression, random forest, and gradient boosting — should be applied to develop predictive attrition models that HR teams can deploy in real-time decision support systems. The inclusion of demographic data and engagement survey results could further enhance predictive power and generate more nuanced, actionable recommendations.

6. Limitations

Several limitations should be considered when interpreting these results. The dataset does not include department or job role information, preventing analysis of sector-specific attrition patterns. The salary variable is categorical (low/medium/high) rather than continuous, limiting the granularity of compensation analysis. Additionally, this is a simulated dataset, and while it reflects realistic workforce data patterns, findings may not generalize directly to specific industries or geographic labor markets. The small number of missing values (n=2 in satisfaction_level) had negligible impact on results.
